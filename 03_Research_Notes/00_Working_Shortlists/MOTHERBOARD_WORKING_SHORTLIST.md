## Chipset Logic

| Chipset | Meaning for this build | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| B650 | Older AM5 value platform. Mature BIOS, usually cheaper. PCIe 5.0 support varies and many boards are Gen4-only for GPU/storage. | Still valid if price is much better. |
| B650E | Older AM5 with stronger PCIe 5.0 support. | Interesting only if priced close to B650/B850. |
| B850 | Newer mainstream AM5. Usually the best 2026 balance: Gen5 M.2, newer networking on good boards, Ryzen 9000-ready positioning. GPU PCIe 5.0 depends on exact board. | Best default tier to research. |
| X870 | Higher connectivity tier, usually USB4 focus and more premium I/O. | Worth it only if price gap is reasonable or USB4 matters. |
| X870E | High-end lane/connectivity tier. | Usually overkill for this build unless price is surprisingly good. |

## What Matters For Roman

Must-have:
- AM5, ATX preferred.
- Stable 2x32GB DDR5-6000 EXPO support.
- Strong enough VRM/heatsinks for Ryzen 9 9900X.
- At least 3 M.2 slots preferred.
- At least one good CPU-connected M.2 slot.
- Good physical clearance around GPU slot for a large RTX 5080.
- BIOS Flashback.
- 2.5G LAN minimum; 5G LAN is nice.
- WiFi 6E or WiFi 7.
- Enough fan headers for airflow case.

Nice-to-have:
- PCIe 5.0 M.2 slot.
- PCIe 5.0 GPU slot, but not required for RTX 5080 value.
- USB-C front-panel header.
- USB4 only if Roman expects fast external SSD/dock workflows.
- Debug LEDs or postcode display.
- Tool-less M.2 latches and GPU release button.

Avoid:
- Very cheap A620/B840 boards for this build.
- Micro-ATX unless the case/price decision changes.
- Boards with weak VRM cooling or poor rear I/O.
- Paying a large premium for X870E just for marketing.
- Choosing by chipset alone without checking exact board specs.

## Light Research Notes

- AMD has been transitioning from B650 toward B850, but B650 remains relevant because prices and stock can still make it a value option.
- B850 is the more natural 2026 mainstream tier for a new Ryzen 9000 build.
- Reviews of midrange B850 boards suggest performance is generally similar to expensive boards when VRM and BIOS are good; board choice is mostly about features, I/O, price, and stability.
- Some X870 boards look amazing on paper but can involve lane-sharing compromises when many M.2/USB4 devices are used. Do not buy X870 just because the model name sounds premium.
